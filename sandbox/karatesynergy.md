### Strength Baseline & Program Calibration

Your SS score of 57.1 understates you — it reflects **detraining from surgery**, not your actual ability. You already know barbell movement patterns well. This program skips the "learn the lift" beginner phase and instead focuses on:

1. **Rebuilding pressing strength** — BB bench press is a primary lift from Day 1, starting at ~67% of your estimated 1RM and progressing toward 90%+ by Phase 3.
2. **Restoring pulling volume** — your deadlift history (150 × 5) means we can work at meaningful loads quickly.
3. **Cautious squat progression** — your knee limits the squat, not your movement skill. We respect that.
4. **Chin-ups from Day 1** — 3 reps at 110 kg BW is legitimately strong. We build from there.

### Why A/B (not ABCD)?

At your training age and frequency, repeating each movement pattern **every week** builds skill and strength faster than rotating four different sessions. The variety comes from **phase changes every 4 weeks**, where exercises, rep ranges, and intensity all shift. This gives you novelty without sacrificing frequency.

## 3. How to Use This Program

### RPE (Rate of Perceived Exertion) Scale

All loads are prescribed by **RPE** — a subjective scale of effort. Even though suggested starting weights are listed (based on your recent numbers), RPE takes priority. If the suggested weight feels heavier or lighter than the target RPE, **adjust in real time**.

| RPE | Meaning | Reps left "in the tank" |
|---|---|---|
| 6 | Moderate — could do 4+ more reps | 4+ |
| 7 | Challenging — could do 3 more reps | 3 |
| 8 | Hard — could do 2 more reps | 2 |
| 9 | Very hard — could do 1 more rep | 1 |
| 10 | Maximum effort — nothing left | 0 |

### Week-to-Week Progression Rules

1. **If you completed all sets and reps at the target RPE → add weight next week.**
   - Barbell compound lifts: +2.5 kg (use fractional plates if available) to +5 kg
   - Dumbbells/Kettlebells: jump to next available weight, or add 1–2 reps first
2. **If you failed to complete reps or RPE was 9–10 → repeat the same load next week.**
3. **If an exercise causes knee pain (sharp, not just discomfort) → substitute from the ACL-safe alternatives (Section 9).**
4. **Week 4 of each phase is a DELOAD** — reduce load by ~20%, keep reps the same, focus on perfect form and speed.

### Session Structure (Every Session)

| Block | Duration | Content |
|---|---|---|
| **Warm-up** | 5 min | Dynamic mobility (see Section 8) |
| **Main pairs** | 22–25 min | 2 superset pairs (A1/A2, B1/B2) |
| **Finisher** | 3–5 min | Core work |
| **Cool-down stretch** | 5–7 min | Static flexibility (see Section 8) |

### Superset Notation

Exercises labeled **A1/A2** are performed back-to-back with minimal rest, then rest before repeating. This saves time and keeps heart rate elevated.

## 7. Exercise ↔ Karate Transfer Map

Understanding **why** each exercise is in the program helps you train with intent.

| Exercise | Karate transfer | Technique supported |
|---|---|---|
| BB Back Squat | Strong, stable stances; absorbing impact | Zenkutsu-dachi, kiba-dachi |
| Romanian Deadlift / Deadlift | Hip hinge power; posterior chain | Mae geri (snap), stability in kicks |
| Bulgarian Split Squat | Single-leg balance, ACL resilience | All kicks from one leg |
| KB Swing | Explosive hip extension, hip snap | Gyaku-zuki power, mawashi geri |
| Hip Thrust | Maximal hip extension force | Oi-zuki lunge, deep stances |
| Single-leg RDL | Balance + hamstring strength | Yoko geri, balance in kicking |
| **BB Bench Press** | **Horizontal pressing power, pec/tricep drive** | **Oi-zuki, choku-zuki, tsuki combinations** |
| Overhead Press / Push Press | Vertical pressing + full body brace | Age-uke, jodan techniques |
| BB Bent-over Row / DB Row | Pulling strength, posture | Hikite (pulling hand), guard |
| Chin-ups | Vertical pulling, lat engagement, grip | Kime (focus/tension at impact), clinch control |
| Dead Bug / Hollow Body | Core rigidity, spinal stability | Kata, kihon stance transitions |
| Side Plank / Pallof Press | Anti-rotation, lateral stability | Resisting rotation on impact |
| KB Rotational Press | Rotational power chain | Gyaku-zuki (reverse punch) |
| KB Snatch / High Pull | Total-body explosive power | General athletic power |

### A Note on Bench Press and Karate

The bench press catches some criticism in martial arts circles ("you don't fight lying down"). But for you specifically, it serves multiple purposes:
1. **Maintaining hard-earned strength** — you've built to 115 kg 1RM. Losing that is neither necessary nor beneficial.
2. **Pec/tricep power** — the same muscles that lock out a punch. Bench press at RPE 8+ with explosive intent trains the exact speed-strength needed for tsuki.
3. **Shoulder health** — a strong, well-controlled bench press (with proper scapular retraction) actually protects the shoulder joint. Weak pressing muscles + repetitive punching = impingement risk.
4. **Physique goal** — bench press is the most efficient chest builder you have access to. It directly supports your definition goal.

## 9. ACL Knee Protocol

Your right knee (ACL reconstruction, 1.5 years post-op) requires ongoing attention. The program is designed to progressively rebuild trust and strength in that leg.

### Built-in Protections

| Strategy | Implementation |
|---|---|
| **Conservative squat start** | Phase 1 starts at 60 kg — well below your capability — to rebuild confidence under controlled tempo |
| **Right leg leads on unilateral work** | Always start step-ups, split squats with the surgical leg |
| **Controlled tempos in Phase 1–2** | Slow eccentrics build tendon resilience and proprioception |
| **Single-leg RDL in Phase 3** | Challenges balance and builds hamstring co-contraction (protects ACL) |
| **No plyometric jumps** | Explosive *intent* with barbell, but no impact landings |
| **Goblet squat as warm-up/fallback** | If barbell squat triggers knee issues on any day, regress to goblet squat |

### Traffic Light System for Knee Feedback

| Signal | Sensation | Action |
|---|---|---|
| 🟢 **Green** | Mild muscle fatigue, slight warmth, normal | Continue training |
| 🟡 **Yellow** | Dull ache, mild instability feeling, stiffness | Reduce load 20%, reduce range of motion, observe next session |
| 🔴 **Red** | Sharp pain, catching/locking, swelling within 24 hrs | Stop exercise. Substitute. If persistent, consult physiotherapist. |

### Substitution Table

If a prescribed exercise triggers 🔴, use this substitute:

| Primary exercise | Substitute |
|---|---|
| BB Back Squat | Goblet Squat (16–28 kg KB) or Box Squat |
| Bulgarian Split Squat | Step-up (lower box) or Reverse Lunge |
| KB Swing | KB Deadlift (no ballistic) |
| Fast BW Squat (warm-up) | Slow bodyweight squat |
| Single-leg RDL | Two-leg RDL or Hamstring curl (band) |

### Weekly Knee Health Check

At the end of each **training week**, honestly assess and note:

| Question | Week ___ |
|---|---|
| Any sharp pain during training? (Y/N) | |
| Swelling after session? (Y/N) | |
| Feeling of instability or "giving way"? (Y/N) | |
| Confidence level on right leg (1–10) | |

Track this alongside your load tracker. If confidence trends **up** over weeks, you're on the right path.

## 12. Nutrition Notes (Brief)

Your goal #3 is **definition**. At 110 kg and 186 cm, you likely carry both significant muscle and excess body fat. Strength training builds the muscle; **nutrition reveals it**.

| Principle | Guideline |
|---|---|
| **Protein** | 1.6–2.0 g per kg bodyweight = **~180–220 g/day**. Prioritize this above all else. |
| **Caloric deficit** | For gradual fat loss without strength loss: ~300–500 kcal/day deficit. No more — you're rebuilding bench strength. |
| **Meal timing** | Eat a protein-rich meal 1–2 hrs before and within 1 hr after training. Especially important on bench day. |
| **Hydration** | 3+ liters/day minimum. More on training days. |
| **Recovery** | 7–9 hours sleep. This is non-negotiable for strength recovery AND karate skill acquisition. |

> At your strength level, you can realistically gain bench press strength while in a moderate deficit for the first several months (muscle memory / neural adaptation). Don't cut too aggressively or bench progress will stall.
